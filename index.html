The problem I'm solving in this workshop

Every student has used ChatGPT. Most have seen it hallucinate. Few understand why, and almost none know how to fix it. The fix isn't better prompts. It's giving AI access to YOUR knowledge base so it answers from real data instead of statistical guesses.

Andrej Karpathy (founding member of OpenAI, former Tesla AI director) recently told developers to build a personal knowledge base and let LLMs read it. He recommended Obsidian with local Markdown files. Smart advice. I took it further with Notion, because my knowledge base needs to work across devices, connect to Claude Code via MCP, and serve as a single source of truth for my entire development workflow.

In this workshop, I build that system live. Students watch me go from an empty Notion workspace to a fully connected AI second brain that answers questions with zero hallucination, because every answer comes from data I put there.

Fair warning: I've had AI confidently cite a Notion page that I deleted two weeks earlier. It generated a perfect summary of content that no longer existed. That failure taught me more about AI grounding than any tutorial, and I'll show students exactly how to prevent it.

Imagine you have a super smart robot friend...

But sometimes your robot friend makes stuff up! You ask "What did I eat for lunch?" and it says "Pizza!" when you actually had a sandwich. That's because the robot is GUESSING instead of KNOWING.

So what if you gave your robot a notebook? A notebook with all YOUR stuff in it. Your homework, your favorite games, your projects. Now when you ask a question, the robot reads YOUR notebook first and gives you the RIGHT answer!

That's what I'm going to build in front of you. A digital notebook (called Notion) that talks to an AI robot (called Claude). By the end, the robot only says things it read from YOUR notebook. No more making stuff up!

Funny story: One time my robot read a page from my notebook that I already threw away. It told me all about something that didn't exist anymore! That's when I learned: even smart robots need good rules.

1

Build a Notion knowledge base

8 min

I open an empty Notion workspace on the projector. In 8 minutes, I build a structured knowledge base: a Projects database, a Research Notes section, and a Code Snippets library, all connected with linked relations and tags. Students see that AI grounding starts with structure. If your knowledge is scattered across random docs, AI can't find it. If it's in a database with relations, AI can query it like SQL.

"I now have a personal wiki with ~300 articles. The LLM can read the full context of each article. No chunking, no fragments, no lost context." Andrej Karpathy, on building an LLM knowledge base

Think of Notion like a super organized backpack. Instead of throwing all your papers in randomly, you have folders: one for projects, one for notes, one for cool code tricks. When everything is neat and labeled, your robot friend can find anything in seconds. If it's messy? The robot gets confused and starts guessing. A very smart man named Andrej (he helped create ChatGPT!) said everyone should build a notebook like this for their AI.

What students learn

  • Notion database architecture
  • Linked relations and rollups
  • Why structure prevents hallucination
  • Karpathy's knowledge base approach
  • Obsidian vs Notion tradeoffs

2

Connect AI to your knowledge (MCP + Notion)

10 min

This is where the magic happens. I edit one JSON file (.mcp.json) and Claude Code connects directly to my Notion workspace via MCP (Model Context Protocol). I ask Claude a question about one of my projects. Instead of guessing, it searches my Notion database, finds the relevant page, and answers from that page only. Students see AI go from "I think..." to "According to your notes on..." in one configuration change.

Karpathy recommended Obsidian because it stores Markdown locally, which LLMs read natively. Notion requires an API bridge, but the tradeoff is worth it: cloud sync, team collaboration, database views, and the MCP connection means Claude can query Notion like a database, not just read flat files.

Now I plug a cable between the robot and the notebook! Well, not a real cable. I type one tiny instruction and suddenly the robot can READ your notebook. Before this, the robot was just guessing. After this, when you ask "What's my science project about?", it opens your notebook, finds the page, and tells you EXACTLY what you wrote. No guessing! It's like giving a student the textbook before the test instead of making them guess.

What students learn

  • MCP (Model Context Protocol)
  • Notion API integration
  • .mcp.json configuration
  • Source-grounded AI answers
  • Why RAG hallucinates but this doesn't
  • Obsidian vs Notion for LLMs

3

Custom Skills and CLAUDE.md rules

10 min

Now I teach AI the rules. I create a CLAUDE.md file with three non-negotiable instructions: only answer from my Notion, always cite the source page URL, and say "I don't have information on that" if the data isn't there. Then I build a reusable Skill called /research that students can use themselves. I type /research "LLM agents" and Claude searches my Notion, finds matching pages, and writes a summary with citations. No hallucination, because it can't make things up if the rules forbid it.

Now I give the robot THREE rules. Rule 1: Only talk about stuff in MY notebook. Rule 2: Always tell me WHICH page you read it from. Rule 3: If you don't know, just say "I don't know." That last rule is the most important. It's like telling your friend: "If you didn't see it, don't pretend you did." Then I make a magic button called "/research" that anyone can press to search the notebook instantly!

What students learn

  • CLAUDE.md project rules
  • Custom Skills (slash commands)
  • Grounding constraints for AI
  • Citation enforcement
  • "I don't know" as a feature
  • Reusable prompt templates

4

Karpathy's Agentic Engineering framework

12 min

This is the conceptual heart of the workshop. I walk through Karpathy's framework for how developers should think about AI in 2026. He calls it "agentic engineering": you're not writing code anymore, you're orchestrating agents and acting as oversight. The skill now is judgment, not syntax.

"I've never felt this much behind as a programmer. The profession is being dramatically refactored. I have a sense that I could be 10X more powerful if I just properly string together what has become available over the last year." Andrej Karpathy, March 2026

I show students the five layers: your knowledge base at the bottom (what we built in Projects 1-3), then intent specification, task decomposition, agent orchestration, and judgment at the top. The point is that technical mastery is "even more of a multiplier than before." A developer who deeply understands architecture can leverage agents for 10x productivity. A beginner just generates broken code faster.

A really famous computer scientist named Andrej built some of the first AI we use today. He says the most important skill now isn't typing code. It's THINKING. Imagine you're the boss of a team of robots. You don't do the work yourself. You tell each robot what to do, check their work, and make sure nobody messes up. That's what coding looks like in 2026. The smarter YOU are, the better your robots work. If you don't understand what they're building, you can't tell when they make a mistake!

What students learn

  • Karpathy's agentic engineering
  • Vibe coding vs real engineering
  • Judgment as the core AI skill
  • Task decomposition for agents
  • Why deep knowledge still matters
  • The 10x multiplier concept

5

Live build: AI writes a grounded report

16 min

Everything comes together. I type /research "multi-agent systems for enterprise" and Claude searches my Notion, finds 5 relevant pages and 12 code snippets, and writes a 2-page research summary with inline citations linking back to the exact Notion pages. Every claim is traceable. Nothing is invented.

Then I do something important: I run the same query through regular Claude (without the Notion connection) and put both outputs side by side. Students see the difference between hallucinated output ("Studies show that...") and grounded output ("According to your notes on March 15..."). That comparison is the entire point of the workshop.

What I'll be honest about

This system isn't perfect. If your Notion is incomplete, the AI's answers will be incomplete too. I once asked it about a project I hadn't documented yet and it returned "No relevant information found." That's actually the correct behavior, but it felt frustrating in the moment. Teaching students that "I don't know" from AI is a feature, not a bug, is one of the hardest lessons.

Now the big finale! I press the magic "/research" button and the robot reads my entire notebook, finds all the pages about a topic, and writes a report with LINKS to where it found everything. You can click any link and see "yep, that's really in the notebook."

Then I do something really cool: I ask the SAME question to a robot WITHOUT the notebook. It makes stuff up! "Studies show that blah blah blah..." but there ARE no studies. I put both answers next to each other on screen. One is honest. One is a liar. Guess which one had the notebook? That's the whole point of this workshop.

A fun thing I learned the hard way

If your notebook is empty about something, the robot says "I don't know." That sounds bad, but it's actually GREAT. It means the robot is being honest instead of faking it. Teaching robots to say "I don't know" is one of the most important things you can do.
